Design Process

Good games don't appear fully formed — they go through planning, testing, and revision. Here's how I approach the iterative design process.

1

Brainstorming & Concept

Every project starts with a question: what do I want the player to feel or learn? For Rainforest Cure, I brainstormed around the theme of environmental awareness. I sketched rough ideas on paper — a maze game, a puzzle game, a runner — and picked the runner because it felt the most energetic and matched the urgency of the environmental message.

2

Game Design Document (GDD)

Before touching Unity, I wrote a short GDD that defined the core loop: run, collect gems, avoid obstacles, restore the forest health bar. I also sketched a rough level layout and decided on the win/lose conditions. Having a document kept me focused when I got stuck during development.

3

Prototype & Playtest

I built a basic version of Rainforest Cure with placeholder assets first. I had classmates playtest it and collected feedback. The main issue was that the difficulty spike was too sudden in the middle section. I adjusted the gem spawn rate and obstacle speed based on that feedback.

4

Refinement & Final Build

After playtesting I added a health bar UI, improved the background art, added sound effects, and wrote a short intro screen explaining the game's message. The final version felt much more polished and the environmental theme came through clearly in both the gameplay and the visuals.

Competitions & Challenges

G4C

Games for Change

Submitted Rainforest Cure to the Games for Change student challenge. The competition challenges students to create games that address real-world social issues. My entry focused on environmental conservation and the importance of protecting rainforests.

What I learned: Competing in G4C taught me how to pitch a game with a clear message. I had to think beyond just "is this fun?" and ask "does this actually say something?" That shift in thinking has changed how I approach every project now.

INFY

InfyAPP Challenge

Competed in the InfyAPP Challenge, run by Infosys Foundation USA at Thomas A. Edison CTE High School. Students build working apps designed to improve New Yorkers' lives, mentored by Infosys volunteers and judged by a panel of industry professionals. My team made it to the semi-finals.

What I learned: Getting to the semi-finals showed me that my work could hold up against real competition. The challenge pushed me to think about who I was building for — not just what the app does, but why someone would actually need it. It was my first time designing with a real user in mind, and that changed how I think about every project since.

Reflection & Goals

What I've Learned

Over the past two years in the Game Design pathway, I've grown from someone who could barely write a Python function to a developer who can build and ship a Unity game with C# scripts, collision systems, UI elements, and audio. The biggest growth hasn't been technical though — it's been learning how to finish things. A half-built game means nothing. I've learned to scope my ideas so I can actually complete them.

Strengths & Challenges

My strengths are in the creative side — coming up with ideas, designing levels, and thinking about what an experience should feel like. My biggest challenge is debugging. When my C# code doesn't work the way I expect, it can take a long time to figure out why. I've gotten better at reading error messages and using Debug.Log to trace problems, but it's still something I'm working on.

Career Goals & Final Statement

My goal is to study computer science or game design in college and eventually work in the game industry — either as a developer or a designer. I'm especially interested in games for social impact, creating experiences that change how people think about real-world problems. The Game Design pathway at Edison has given me real tools, real challenges, and real projects I can point to and say: I built that. That's the foundation I'm bringing into senior year and beyond.
